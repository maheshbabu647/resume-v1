## Why the Education Section Matters More at This Stage

There's a reason the Education section appears at the top of an Indian fresher resume — right after the objective — instead of at the bottom where experienced professionals put it.

For someone with three or four years of work history, education is background context. The work history carries the weight.

For a fresher, education **is** the primary qualification. It's what confirms you are eligible to apply, what gives the recruiter confidence that you have the foundational knowledge the role requires, and what the ATS uses to verify your degree, branch, and academic standing.

Most large Indian IT companies — TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL, Cognizant — screen applicants against academic eligibility criteria before a human ever reads the resume. Your CGPA and percentage across your 10th, 12th, and degree determine whether you pass that filter. The Education section is where those numbers live.

Get this section right. It doesn't take long, but the details matter.

---

## What the Education Section Must Include

Every entry in your Education section needs these elements at minimum:

**Degree name** — Written in full. Not "B.Tech" alone — "B.Tech in Computer Science Engineering." Not "MBA" alone — "Master of Business Administration (Marketing)." The full degree name with specialisation is what the ATS uses to categorise your qualification and match it against the degree requirement in the JD.

**Institution name** — The full, official name of your college or university. Not an abbreviation unless the full name is widely unwieldy. "XYZ College of Engineering, Hyderabad" is better than just "XYZ College."

**Graduating year** — The year you graduated or are expected to graduate. For ongoing degrees: "2021–2025 (Expected May 2025)" or simply "Expected: May 2025." Don't leave this blank — ATS systems use graduation year to calculate recency and determine batch eligibility.

**CGPA or Percentage** — Covered in detail in the next section.

**Board name (for 10th and 12th)** — CBSE, ICSE, State Board (name the state), etc.

## The CGPA Question: When to Include It, When Not To, How to Format It

This is the question every fresher asks, and the answer has nuance.

---

### When to include your CGPA

**Include it if your CGPA is 7.0 or above on a 10-point scale** (or equivalent 65%+ on a percentage scale). At this level, your academic performance is an asset that adds to your profile.

**Include it even if it's between 6.0 and 6.9** — you should still show it. Most large IT companies require a minimum of 6.0 CGPA (60%) across all three levels: 10th, 12th, and graduation. Showing 6.2 or 6.5 confirms eligibility, which matters more than looking impressive.

**Do not omit your CGPA to hide it.** Recruiters will notice a missing CGPA and assume the worst. More importantly, background checks at companies like TCS, Infosys, and Wipro verify academic records. A number discovered during verification that doesn't match your resume is a much worse outcome than a mediocre CGPA that was disclosed upfront.

---

### When you can omit it

**Below 6.0 CGPA** — If your CGPA is below the eligibility cutoff for your target companies, you can choose not to list it. However, you should be applying to companies that don't have strict CGPA filters in the first place — startups, tier-2 companies, product companies, and smaller organisations tend to weight skills and projects more heavily than academic scores. For those applications, a strong Skills and Projects section can compensate. But dishonesty — fabricating or significantly rounding up your CGPA — is never the answer.

**A note on rounding:** TCS NQT explicitly states that no rounding is allowed. A 59.9% does not qualify as 60%. Do not round your CGPA upward and present it as meeting a cutoff it doesn't meet.

---

### How to format your CGPA

Use the format your institution uses. Most Indian universities use a 10-point scale:

```
CGPA: 8.2 / 10
```

If your university also provides a percentage equivalent and the recruiter or application form asks for percentage, you can add it:

```
CGPA: 8.2 / 10 (Equivalent: 77.9%)
```

If your university uses percentage (common for older universities or state boards):

```
Percentage: 78.4%
```

If your university uses a 7-point or 4-point scale, mention the scale clearly:

```
CGPA: 3.7 / 4.0
```

Consistency matters. Use the same format across all entries.

## Should You Include 10th and 12th Marks?

In India, this is uniquely important for fresher applications — far more so than in most other countries.

Companies like TCS and Infosys check 10th and 12th marks as part of their eligibility criteria. TCS NQT requires a minimum of 60% across all academic levels — 10th, 12th, and every semester of graduation. Infosys requires a minimum of 60% in 10th and 12th alongside degree eligibility. Many other IT companies follow similar patterns.

This means: **for campus placement and off-campus drives targeting large IT companies, include your 10th and 12th marks.**

**When to include them:**

- When applying to TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL, Cognizant, Accenture, or similar mass recruiters for fresher roles — always include
- When your 10th/12th scores are above 70% — they add positive signal
- When you recently graduated (within 2–3 years) — standard expectation for fresher profiles

**When you can leave them out:**

- When applying to startups, product companies, or roles where eligibility criteria focus on skills rather than academic scores
- When you have a strong degree CGPA and the 10th/12th scores are not requested by the company
- When space is tight on your one-page resume and your degree CGPA is strong — in this case, degree CGPA is more important

**A practical rule:** If you are applying to companies that explicitly ask for 10th and 12th marks, include them. If the company or role doesn't mention it, exercise judgment based on whether the scores help or hurt your profile and how much space you have.

## The Full Structure: What Each Entry Should Look Like

### For your degree (most space, most detail)

```
B.Tech in Computer Science Engineering
XYZ College of Engineering, Hyderabad | 2021 – 2025 | CGPA: 8.2 / 10
Relevant Coursework: Data Structures, DBMS, Computer Networks, 
                     Operating Systems, Object-Oriented Programming
Academic Achievement: Department rank 3 (2024); Dean's List 2023
```

### For 12th (briefer)

```
Class XII — CBSE | ABC School, Hyderabad | 2021 | 88%
```

### For 10th (briefest)

```
Class X — CBSE | ABC School, Hyderabad | 2019 | 91%
```

The degree entry gets the most real estate. The 10th and 12th entries are compact — the critical information (board, institution, year, marks) fits in one line each.

## Relevant Coursework: An Underused Keyword Opportunity

This is the most underused element of the Education section on Indian fresher resumes, and it's a missed opportunity for both ATS matching and demonstrating domain depth.

Here is why it matters.

When an ATS scans your resume, it looks for keyword matches across all sections. Your Skills section and Projects section carry the most keywords. But your Education section can contribute too — specifically through the names of relevant courses you completed.

A data analyst JD that mentions "Statistics" and "Database Management" — if your coursework list includes "Statistics and Probability" and "DBMS" — those are additional keyword matches your resume picks up that most other candidates miss.

Beyond keywords: a course list tells the recruiter what foundation you built during your degree. It's particularly useful for roles where the degree branch alone doesn't tell the full story — an ECE graduate applying for an IT role can signal through relevant coursework that they have the CS-adjacent training the role requires.

**How to choose what to list:**

- List 4–6 courses maximum — enough to be meaningful, not so many that the section looks padded
- Choose courses that are directly relevant to the role or JD you're targeting
- Use the formal course name as it appears on your transcript — "Data Structures and Algorithms" is better than just "DSA" because it matches more keyword variations
- Tailor this per application — for a data role, list Statistics, Machine Learning, DBMS; for a networking role, list Computer Networks, Network Security; for a mechanical design role, list CAD, Thermodynamics, Machine Design

**What not to list:** Generic courses every engineering student takes ("Engineering Mathematics," "English Communication," "Environmental Science") add no keyword value and use space that could carry stronger information.

## Academic Achievements: What Belongs and How to Write It

If you have meaningful academic achievements, they belong in the Education section rather than in a separate section.

**What counts as a meaningful academic achievement:**

- University rank or department rank (e.g., "Department rank 3 out of 180 students")
- Dean's List or academic merit list
- Gold medal, silver medal, or any academic award
- Scholarship received for academic performance
- Paper published or presented at a conference while studying
- Best project award at university or state level

**What doesn't add value:**

- "Scored above average in most subjects" — vague, no reference point
- "Active participation in college events" — that belongs in Extracurriculars, not Education
- Course completion certificates without academic standing — those go in Certifications

**How to format achievements concisely:**

```
Academic Achievement: Secured Department Rank 3 out of 180 students 
                      (2023-24); Recipient of Institute Merit 
                      Scholarship for academic year 2022-23
```

One to two lines. Specific. Verifiable.

## The Reverse Chronological Rule

Education is always listed in reverse chronological order — most recent qualification first, working backwards.

This means:
1. MBA / M.Tech / M.Sc (if applicable)
2. B.Tech / B.E / BCA / B.Com / B.Sc (your primary degree)
3. Class XII (12th standard)
4. Class X (10th standard)

Do not put 10th before 12th, and do not put 12th before your degree. The recruiter reads top to bottom and needs to encounter your highest qualification first.

---

## What to Leave Out

**Your full home address or city of your school** — City is fine; full address is not needed.

**Marks from before Class 10** — If you're listing a 9th standard mark or earlier, stop. It serves no purpose.

**CGPA below 6.0 for companies with a 6.0 cutoff** — If you're applying to a company that has a known eligibility cutoff you don't meet, do not include your CGPA as if you do meet it. Either apply to companies where you are eligible, or apply to companies without strict CGPA filters and omit the score.

**Long lists of irrelevant courses** — "Environmental Science and Engineering," "Engineering Mathematics I, II, III, IV" — if it doesn't add keyword value and doesn't demonstrate domain relevance, leave it out. Space on a one-page resume is limited.

**Awards that are vague or unverifiable** — "Contributed to college activities" is not an academic achievement. "Secured 3rd place in intercollegiate technical quiz" is — but it belongs in Extracurriculars, not Education.

## ATS and the Education Section: What It's Looking For

ATS systems parse the Education section looking for specific data points:

**Degree level** — Did the candidate complete an undergraduate degree? Postgraduate? Does it match the JD requirement?

**Degree name and branch** — "Computer Science Engineering" matches a JD requirement for "CS/IT background" better than "Computer Science" alone. Use the full, formal name.

**Institution name** — Some companies filter by institution. IITs, NITs, and some private institutes may be prioritised by certain recruiters. Make sure your college name is complete and spelled correctly.

**Graduation year** — Used to determine batch eligibility (2025 batch, 2024 batch, etc.) and to verify recency.

**Academic scores** — Used for eligibility screening against minimum CGPA/percentage cutoffs.

**Coursework keywords** — Parsed as supplementary keyword matches against the JD.

For all of these to parse correctly, your Education section must use standard plain text — no tables, no graphical elements, no text boxes. A single-column plain-text layout with the degree on one line and institution/year/CGPA on the next is the most reliably parsed format.

## The Education Section Checklist

```
STRUCTURE
□ Listed in reverse chronological order (most recent first)
□ Degree entry has the most space; 10th/12th are compact
□ All entries are in plain text — no tables, no icons

CONTENT — DEGREE
□ Full degree name with specialisation included
□ Full college/university name
□ Graduation year (or expected graduation date for final year)
□ CGPA or percentage (in the correct format for your institution)
□ 4–6 relevant course names from your degree
□ Academic achievements, if any (rank, medal, scholarship)

CONTENT — 12TH
□ Board name (CBSE / ICSE / State Board + state)
□ School name
□ Year of passing
□ Percentage or marks

CONTENT — 10TH
□ Board name
□ School name
□ Year of passing
□ Percentage or marks

ATS
□ Degree name matches (or is compatible with) the JD's 
  stated educational requirement
□ Coursework names include terms that appear in the JD
□ CGPA is presented in the format the company application 
  asks for
□ No tables, borders, or images in this section
```
