# Resume for Students With Low CGPA in India — How to Compete When Numbers Don't Help

**By CareerForge.pro** | Resume Advice for Indian Freshers | 13 min read

---

> *A low CGPA closes some doors.*
> *It does not close all of them.*
> *And the ones it closes are not always the ones worth walking through.*
>
> *This blog is about what you do with the doors that are still open.*

---

## The Honest Starting Point

Before anything else, let's be direct about what a low CGPA means in the Indian job market in 2026 — because pretending the numbers don't matter would be dishonest, and pretending they determine everything would be equally wrong.

**What a low CGPA actually affects:**

Companies like TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL, and Cognizant use academic eligibility cutoffs to manage the volume of applications they receive at campus drives and national recruitment tests. Their stated minimums are typically 60% or 6.0 CGPA across 10th, 12th, and graduation — and these are applied consistently. If you are below these thresholds, the ATS or campus portal will filter you out before any human reviews your application.

This is a real constraint. Acknowledging it is not pessimism — it is the starting point for building a strategy that actually works.

**What a low CGPA does not affect:**

- Startups and product companies that evaluate candidates on skills, projects, and problem-solving ability
- Tier-2 and tier-3 IT companies with more flexible eligibility criteria
- Companies that hire through AMCAT, eLitmus, or CoCubes assessments where your test score is the primary filter
- Off-campus applications where you apply directly through company portals and are screened on skills rather than campus-drive rules
- Any role where your GitHub profile, live project, or certification demonstrates ability more credibly than your transcript does

The strategy for a low-CGPA fresher is not to hide the number — it is to build a profile where the number becomes secondary to the evidence of what you can actually do.

---

## First: What Counts as "Low CGPA" in Different Contexts

"Low" means different things depending on what you're targeting.

**Below 6.0 / 60%:** Below the eligibility threshold for most large IT service companies at campus drives. This is where the constraint is most acute. Off-campus is your primary route.

**6.0 to 6.9 / 60–69%:** You meet the minimum for most IT service companies but fall below the "competitive range" for top-tier companies and some product companies. You're eligible — but you need the rest of your profile to work harder.

**7.0 to 7.4 / 70–74%:** Eligible for most companies. Competitive range for IT services. May fall short for companies like Infosys DSE or Wipro Turbo tracks that prefer higher scores, but not a significant barrier for most roles.

**This blog focuses primarily on the two genuinely challenging bands: below 6.0, and 6.0 to 6.9.**

## The Resume Strategy: What Changes and What Doesn't

Here is the core insight: a low CGPA does not change what a good resume looks like. It changes where the CGPA appears, what surrounds it, and which companies you target.

The format from Blog 2 still applies. ATS compliance from Blog 4 still applies. Keywords from Blog 5 still apply. Projects from Blog 7 still apply. What changes is emphasis and targeting.

## Part 1: On the Resume Itself

### Should You Include Your CGPA?

The question most low-CGPA freshers ask first.

**If your CGPA is between 6.0 and 6.9:** Include it. You are above the minimum for most IT service companies. Hiding it raises more questions than showing it — recruiters notice a missing CGPA and assume the worst. A clearly stated 6.4 with strong projects and certifications is a better profile than a resume with a suspicious blank where the CGPA should be.

**If your CGPA is below 6.0:** This requires more nuance.

For companies with a 6.0 cutoff — you don't meet their eligibility threshold regardless of what your resume says. Including a below-threshold CGPA in your application to those companies doesn't help because the cutoff disqualifies you at the portal level before a human reads the document. The more important decision is to redirect your applications to companies that don't have strict CGPA filters.

For companies and roles without a stated CGPA cutoff — startups, small IT firms, many off-campus roles — you may choose to omit the CGPA from the resume and lead with your skills and projects. This is not misrepresentation; it is a presentation choice. However, be aware that if you progress to an interview, the CGPA will likely come up and you need to be prepared to discuss it honestly. Do not fabricate a number — background checks are real and the consequences of falsification are severe and lasting.

**Never round up your CGPA to meet a cutoff it doesn't meet.** A 5.9 is not a 6.0. TCS explicitly states that no rounding is permitted in their eligibility criteria. The same applies across companies that use academic cutoffs. This was covered in Blog 9 — it bears repeating here because it's a temptation for low-CGPA candidates specifically.

---

### How to Position the CGPA on the Resume

When you include a low CGPA, placement matters.

The standard position — after your degree name, on the same line — is correct. Don't bury it at the bottom of the Education section in small text. That signals you're trying to hide it, which creates a worse impression than simply stating it clearly.

What you can do: ensure that strong content appears before and immediately after the Education section. Your objective should lead with your skills and a proof point — not your CGPA. Your Skills section should follow Education with a keyword-dense, specific list. Your Projects section should immediately demonstrate that you built real things using relevant tools.

The sequence should make the recruiter think: *this candidate has done interesting work* — before they reach the CGPA line. Not: *let me check if this CGPA is good enough, and if it isn't, I'll stop reading.*

---

### Strengthen Every Other Section to Compensate

This is the most important strategic point in this blog. A low CGPA means every other section of your resume must be stronger than average — because you are asking the reviewer to weigh your demonstrated skills more heavily than your academic score.

**Projects:** This is your most powerful compensating signal. A fresher with a 6.2 CGPA who has built a functional, deployed web application with 200 users and a clean GitHub repository presents a more compelling case than one with an 8.0 CGPA and three vague project lines. Build real things. Describe them specifically. Link to your GitHub.

**Certifications:** Google, AWS, NPTEL, HackerRank — certifications from recognised issuers demonstrate that you've acquired specific skills. A Google Data Analytics Certificate or an AWS Cloud Practitioner credential provides a credibility signal independent of your academic score.

**AMCAT / eLitmus / HackerRank scores:** Assessment-based hiring platforms give you a way to demonstrate aptitude and technical ability independently of your CGPA. A strong AMCAT score can open doors at companies that use AMCAT as a primary filter rather than CGPA. If your AMCAT quantitative score is in the 85th percentile, that number belongs on your resume — it speaks directly to the ability that CGPA was supposed to measure.

**GitHub activity:** For technical roles, a GitHub profile with regular commits, well-documented repositories, and a visible coding history communicates ongoing technical engagement. This is especially relevant for product company and startup applications.

---

### How to List AMCAT, eLitmus, or CoCubes Scores on Your Resume

If you've taken an assessment platform test and scored well, include it in your Certifications section or create a short Assessments section.

```
ASSESSMENTS

AMCAT — Overall Score: 640 | Quantitative: 85th percentile
eLitmus Certified — pH Test Score: 72/100
HackerRank — 5-star Python, 4-star Java
```

A strong AMCAT score signals to recruiters at companies that use AMCAT (Mphasis, Hexaware, Capgemini, etc.) that you meet their ability threshold — regardless of your CGPA.

## Part 2: The Targeting Strategy — Where to Apply

This is where the real leverage is. A low-CGPA fresher who applies exclusively to companies with strict CGPA cutoffs will struggle. A low-CGPA fresher with a strong skills profile who targets companies without strict cutoffs will find significantly more traction.

---

### Companies and Channels With More Flexible CGPA Criteria

**Tier-2 IT companies:**
Mphasis, Hexaware, Mastech, Sonata Software, Cyient, KPIT, L&T Infotech, Persistent Systems, Zensar Technologies, Birlasoft, Mindtree — these companies hire in volume and many do not apply the same strict cutoffs as TCS or Infosys. Eligibility criteria vary by batch and drive — verify per notification.

**AMCAT-based hiring:**
Companies that recruit through AMCAT (including Capgemini, Mphasis, Tech Mahindra for some roles, and many mid-size IT firms) use AMCAT scores rather than CGPA as the primary filter. A strong test performance opens doors independently of academic scores.

**Startups:**
Early-stage and growth-stage startups in India almost universally prioritise skills and attitude over CGPA. A fresher who can demonstrate they've built something useful — a live project, a tool with users, an open-source contribution — is far more interesting to a startup founder than academic grades.

**Product companies (selective):**
Large product companies like Amazon, Google, and Microsoft set their own hiring criteria and do not always use CGPA as a hard filter. Demonstrated technical ability — competitive programming, strong projects, GitHub — matters more. However, these are also highly competitive roles, so the bar for demonstrated skill is correspondingly higher.

**Off-campus applications through company portals:**
Many companies that have CGPA cutoffs for campus drives apply different (or no) cutoffs for off-campus applications made through their own career portals. Apply directly. Your resume and skills are evaluated on their own merits.

**Internshala and similar platforms:**
Internshala, LetsIntern, and similar platforms list roles from smaller companies and startups where skills and willingness to work are the primary criteria. Many companies here explicitly do not filter by CGPA.

---

### The CGPA Question in Interviews

If you are shortlisted for an interview — which will happen if your skills profile is strong — your CGPA will come up. Be prepared with an honest, composed answer.

The question usually takes the form: "Your CGPA is on the lower side — can you explain that?"

**What works:**
- Be honest about what happened (personal circumstances, wrong focus, academic difficulty in certain semesters) without excessive explanation
- Pivot immediately to what you did about it — the certifications you completed, the projects you built, the skills you developed independently
- Let the skills speak — "I recognised early that I learn better through building things than through exams, which is why I focused on [project/certification]. I'm confident that's reflected in what you can see on my GitHub."

**What doesn't work:**
- Blaming professors, college infrastructure, or the education system — even if legitimate, this signals you're not taking ownership
- Being defensive or apologetic — it draws attention to the number rather than redirecting to capability
- Pretending the CGPA doesn't matter — the interviewer is asking because it matters to them

One honest sentence about the cause, followed by concrete evidence of what you built and learned outside of marks — that is the complete answer. Then stop and let them move on.

## Part 3: The Long-Term Perspective

This is worth stating clearly: **the importance of CGPA in your career diminishes sharply after your first job.**

Once you have professional work experience — even 6–12 months — your work history carries the weight. No one asking for your resume at your second or third job is significantly influenced by your undergraduate CGPA. Companies screening experienced candidates for mid-level roles care about what you built, what you led, what you delivered — not what your 3rd-semester thermodynamics grade was.

The difficulty of a low CGPA is concentrated in the first job search. Get the first job — at any company where your skills are valued — do good work, and the CGPA conversation largely ends there.

This doesn't make the first job search easy. But it does mean the constraint is time-limited.

## The Complete Low-CGPA Fresher Resume: What It Looks Like

Everything from the core series applies. The differences are in the objective framing, the CGPA placement, and the emphasis.

**Objective — lead with skills, not apology:**

```
B.Tech Computer Science graduate with hands-on experience 
in Python, machine learning, and data analysis. Completed 
Google Data Analytics Certificate (2025) and built a crop 
disease detection system with 84% model accuracy. Seeking 
a Data Analyst or Software Developer role to contribute 
to data-driven application development.
```

The CGPA does not appear in the objective. The skills and proof point do. The CGPA appears where it belongs — in the Education section, stated clearly, not hidden.

**Education — stated honestly, surrounded by strength:**

```
EDUCATION

B.Tech in Computer Science Engineering
ABC College of Engineering, Hyderabad | 2022 – 2026 | CGPA: 6.2 / 10
Relevant Coursework: Data Structures, Python Programming, 
                     DBMS, Machine Learning, Computer Networks
```

Clear. Not buried. Not inflated. Surrounded by coursework that demonstrates domain relevance.

**Projects — the centrepiece:**

Two or three well-described projects with technologies, outcomes, and GitHub links. This section does the most work on a low-CGPA resume. It must be excellent. Refer to Blog 7 for the complete framework.

**Assessments (if strong):**

```
ASSESSMENTS

AMCAT — Quantitative Ability: 83rd percentile
HackerRank — 5-star Python (Basic)
```

## The Action Plan: What to Do Right Now

If your CGPA is low and you're preparing for your job search, this is the priority order:

```
1. Build or strengthen your strongest project 
   (if it doesn't have a GitHub link, add one)

2. Complete one recognised certification 
   (Google, AWS, NPTEL, HackerRank — whichever 
   is most relevant to your target role)

3. Take AMCAT if you haven't — a strong score 
   opens off-campus opportunities at multiple 
   companies simultaneously

4. Build your GitHub profile — active commits, 
   pinned repos with READMEs, consistent activity

5. Identify your target company list — focus on 
   companies without strict CGPA filters rather 
   than mass-applying to companies with 6.0+ cutoffs

6. Tailor your resume to each JD using the 
   keyword process from Blog 5

7. Apply directly through company career portals 
   — not just through campus drives

8. Prepare for the CGPA interview question 
   before your first shortlist comes
```

## The Checklist: Low-CGPA Resume Before Submission

```
CGPA HANDLING
□ CGPA is stated honestly — not rounded up, not fabricated
□ CGPA appears in Education section, not in objective
□ Objective leads with skills and a proof point, 
  not with CGPA or apologies for it

COMPENSATING SIGNALS
□ 2–3 projects with specific outcomes and GitHub links
□ At least 1 recognised certification (Google, AWS, 
  NPTEL, HackerRank)
□ AMCAT/eLitmus score included if strong (80th+ percentile)
□ Skills section is specific, keyword-dense, 
  and matches target JD

TARGETING
□ Company list focuses on firms without strict CGPA cutoffs
□ Not mass-applying to mass recruiters with 6.0 cutoffs 
  if below that threshold
□ At least some applications through direct company 
  career portals, not just campus drives

FORMAT
□ Standard ATS-compliant format — Blog 2 guidelines
□ One page
□ No photo, no DOB
□ Notepad test passes

HONESTY
□ No CGPA inflated or rounded to meet a cutoff
□ No skills listed that cannot be discussed in interview
□ No project metrics fabricated
```
